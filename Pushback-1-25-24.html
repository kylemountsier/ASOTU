May Day 2028

Shawn Fain, the fiery leader of the United Auto Workers, is stirring up a storm with his call for a colossal general strike in May 2028, aiming to unite workers across America.

🚩  Strike Call Echoes Through Labor Ranks — In a charged address at the UAW national political conference, Fain didn't just talk shop. He lit a fuse for action, urging members to unite for a massive walkout in 2028. "We want everybody walking out," he proclaimed, setting sights on a nationwide demonstration akin to those in other countries.

🔨  A Master Plan for Maximum Impact — The UAW has smartly lined up its major contract expirations with the U.S.'s big three automakers for May 1, 2028. The date, known globally as International Solidarity Day, is chosen for maximum effect, as Fain nudges other unions to join this synchronized labor showdown.

👥  Fain's Political Punch — After much consideration, the union finally decided to endorse President Biden for re-election during a union conference in Washington D.C. yesterday.

USPS Goes Electric

USPS

The United States Postal Service is not just delivering mail anymore; they're now delivering a statement on sustainability with their latest green initiative.

🚐  The New Mail Carriers on the Block — Say goodbye to the old-school mail vans and hello to USPS's shiny fleet of Ford E-Transits. Funded by the Inflation Reduction Act, these babies boast triple the cargo capacity of their predecessors.

🔌  Charging Up the Future — USPS is sparking a revolution at its South Atlanta hub with new EV charging stations. Manufactured by Siemens, these chargers are like overnight powerhouses getting the fleet ready for eco-friendly deliveries at the crack of dawn.

🌿  Green Fleet Goals — The postal service’s ambitious plan is to roll out about 9,250 by the end of 2024, and 45K by the end of 2028. Ultimately, they intend to have about 66K in their fleet.

Tesla

Tesla is slated to expand further into the EV market with plans to launch a new, affordable model codenamed "Redwood.”

Hybrids Hit the Sweet Spot for Buyers

Giphy/TLC

Electric vehicles have been stealing the limelight for the past several years, but it’s hybrids that have recently emerged as the savvy middle-ground choice for consumers. 

Americans bought a record 1.2M EVs last year, a gain of about 46% and a 7.6% share of all new car sales, according to KBB. But hybrid sales rose even faster — up 65% to more than 1.2M and lifting their market share to 8%.

Why the sudden hype for hybrids? Well, it turns out that EVs come with some, shall we say, complexities. Sky-high prices and the eternal scavenger hunt for charging stations come to mind and are making car buyers think twice before committing to an all-electric vehicle.

 

S&P Global Mobility

 

Plus, these days it seems the price tag speaks louder than eco-credentials, and hybrids are playing the affordability card well, with many models starting around the $30,000 mark.

EVs? They're still hobnobbing in that primo luxury territory. 

 

Edmunds/CNBC

 

In the grand scheme of things, hybrids are like the bridge over troubled waters — a necessary detour on the road to an all-electric future. They're a pragmatic choice, reducing emissions without asking drivers to change their entire lifestyle.

As for EVs, they're still the endgame for most automakers. But hybrids are proving that sometimes the road most traveled is also the one most sensible.
