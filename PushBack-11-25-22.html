AUTOMOTIVE
New Car Demand Dips
 
 
Due to high prices and rising interest, US new vehicle sales are on track for a 0.3% year-over-year dip in November.
 
Average monthly payments are up 7.2% to about $712.
 
Even with a November dip, new vehicle sales for the year are up by nearly 1M. Almost 14M VS. nearly 13M at the same point last year.
 
JD Power says new car demand, price, and profits may show flattening or decline into 2023.
 
Telling it like it is - Interest, prices, and monthly payments are testing new car demand. If only there was some alternative to new cars that people would buy to meet their needs in the meantime… #IfOnly
AUTOMOTIVE
Trucks, Trucks, and Trucks
 
 
GMC's Sierra EV pickup will start under $50K. A surprise since it was announced last month at $107K.
 
What changed? Trim. You can still get the decked-out $107K version, but the bulk of the trucks will be at the more accessible $50K level.
 
 
Tesla's Cybertruck crossed 1.5M pre-orders. With 250K reservations announced just a week after the 2019 reveal, the company has received hundreds a week since.
 
Worth the hype? Hard to guess. Tesla so far has established a pattern of one-upping itself with each release, but with expectations and Musk's hyperbolic hype so high, only time will tell.
 
 
Stellantis recalls 250K Ram pickups in the US. The heavy-duty diesel models could leak transmission fluid and cause engine fires.
 
Don't they stress-test these things anymore? Sure! This particular issue requires a mix of heat and pressure to cause a small leak and for that leaking fluid to land on a hot part of the engine. 16 fires and 48 complaints have been reported.
 
TL; DR - GMC’s EV Truck gets a base model, Tesla’s Cybertruck remains popular, and Stellantis recalls some diesel trucks.
MARKETS
Free Returns Come To An End?
 
 
Apparently, two-thirds of Americans have ordered too many items from online retailers intending to send some back after trying them in person.
 
Not us, of course! 😉
 
Now, some retailers are preparing to charge fees for returns to offset the expense. A study found 10% of the $761B in returns last year were fraudulent.
 
This year the number of large retailers charging fees for returns increased from 31% to 40%.
 
Telling it like it is - This seems like a fair ask, but only if we ignore the mind-boggling diversity between sizes across brands.
