119 Years Of Ford
 
 
Trivia time. What US automaker was incorporated on this day 119 years ago? If, by default, you guessed Ford, you are right! The OEM has become a part of American culture in its long history, and we wanted to bring some interesting facts to you on this occasion.
 
🥇The first Ford dealer was William L. Hughson, who opened his dealership, Standard Motor Car Co. in San Francisco, CA, on January 15, 1903.
 
🏆The oldest operating dealership in the WORLD is Tenvoorde Ford in St. Cloud, MN. The company was founded in February 1903 by Steve Tenvoorde and is now under the 5th generation of Tenvoorde leadership.
 
🚲From 4hp "Quadricycle" to 426hp F-150 Lightnings, Ford has maintained the roles of innovator, economic powerhouse, and cultural icon in various combinations over the last 119 years. Each step of the way, dealerships have played an essential role in the company's success, being representatives in towns and neighborhoods across the nation and over the years.

Cybertruck Falls Behind In EV Truck Race
 
 
Once the only EV truck in town, Tesla has lost its headstart. Musk has delayed the release of the Cybertruck for some time as legacy, and other startup automakers have claimed part of the EV truck market.
 
Still waiting. Current projections have the Cybertruck entering production in 2023 while Ford, GM, Polestar, and Stellantis are already delivering vehicles.
 
Poker tip —  When you show your hand and everybody else goes all in it means they are confident or bluffing.

Nio Launches SUV
 
 
Chinese EV maker, Nio, announced a new SUV with a 300-mile battery range. The company is in a heated contest with Tesla for the Chinese EV market, but news of the SUV bumped Nio stock by 4%.
 
Preorders for the SUV have begun with delivery expected in August. The basic model costs $69,693, with an extended range version running about $4K more.
 
Telling it like it is —  3 months from announcement to delivery seems crazy fast… guess they didn’t get the 2030 memo?

WORLD NEWS
Japanese Automakers Shouldering Inflation Burden
 
 
In a reversal of past trends, Japanese automakers are trying to decrease the impact of inflation by taking on more expenses. Historically OEMs like Toyota and Nissan would protect their bottom line by paying raw material and part suppliers based on inflation. Now they are focusing efforts on protecting suppliers for the long haul.
 
Telling it like it is —  Japanese automakers are displaying how drawing a bigger circle is essential to thriving in the long run.
