Breakdown: New FTC Regulations
 
 
Let's say, theoretically, you do not follow Steve Greenfield on LinkedIn. First, do you even care about being prepared to overcome any obstacles heading your way? Second, you should check out his recent breakdown of the new FTC auto dealer regulations.
 
No-Nos. The regulations ban selling products that provide "no value" and misleading pricing ads while requiring additional pricing disclosures and an online list of add-on products with prices. The plan also limits how dealerships can communicate with consumers and requires additional effort for consumers who want to opt-in for protection products.
 
Price and the public. Steve's short, powerful post also points out that the FTC expects compliance to cost between $1.4b and $1.6b over ten years. Of 1,649 public comments analyzed by Automotive Ventures, less than 1% favored reconsidering or delaying the regulations.

Tesla Breaks 3 Million Cars Produced
 
 
Tesla has produced over 3 million EVs since its founding in 2003. While some legacy OEMs could produce that in a single quarter, Tesla's achievement comes packed with some interesting numbers.
 
1/3 of the vehicles were produced in China. Production in China started in 2018 when Tesla opened the first factory wholly owned by a foreign automaker.
 
Q2 was down 15% from Q1's global production despite June's 25% year-over-year increase.
 
50% annual sales growth remains Musk's prediction for the year. Shares in the company are down 25% so far for 2022.
 
TL:DR — Tesla production has been increasing rapidly since its factory in China opened. Lockdowns in the country have a direct and severe impact on the company. Musk is still swinging for the fences.

BMW And Toyota’s Green Partnership
 
 
Back in 2013, BMW and Toyota took their friendship to the next level. No, they didn't start a band; they partnered to produce green vehicle technology. The pair have collaborated on several cars and worked toward hydrogen-powered vehicle technology along the way. Now they expect to begin selling jointly-developed hydrogen vehicles as soon as 2025.
 
Why hydrogen? While many brands and consumers are moving toward EVs, BMW and Toyota see hydrogen as an alternative to EVs' long recharge times and supply chain shortage-prone production risks.
 
Telling it like it is —  Don’t put all your eggs in one basket. Or, in this case - Don't fill all your baskets with the same kind of eggs…?
