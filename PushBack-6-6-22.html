An Open Letter To Jim Farley
 
 
Ford CEO Jim Farley announced the company will sell EVs 100% online. The plan will convert all existing dealerships into high-quality service centers with no inventory on the ground. The news contrasts previous claims that Ford sees the dealership model as the best way to serve customers.
 
Many are hoping for clarity in the wake of the public announcement, including Brian Pasch. He published an open letter to Farley on LinkedIn.
 
In the letter, Pasch asks some clarifying questions, which we have done our best to summarize here:
  1. How will the shift to the agency model change the dealer's required investment in franchising?
  2. Who is going to work with customers with upside-down trades or poor credit?
  3. What happens to the robust trade-in/used car business? 
  4. Will certified pre-owned EVs be direct to consumers as well? 
  5. What is Ford's risk tolerance for the build-to-order model? US consumers are not used to waiting for vehicles to be built and may go across the street to a different dealer with vehicles they can buy. 
  6. What happens to fixed pricing when national inventory is not sold, or orders fail to meet production goals?
  7. Will Ford also offer customers F&I, or will local dealers still have the ability to offer financing? 
  8. Has Ford calculated the agency model's impact on dealer profit margins? 
  9. Will the increased complexity and options in ordering a Ford vs. Tesla be worth it to customers?
  10. Who is going to build the sales portal? 
Telling it like it is —  Brian Pasch is the best kind of troublemaker! We want to know your thoughts on the Ford announcement and the questions posed by Brian’s open letter. Reply to this email and let us know what you think.

MONEY AND MARKETS
Retail Inventory Surplus?
 
 
Five seconds after we all agreed the inventory shortage is here for a bit, the retail goods industry had to act out. Big box stores like Target, Walmart, and Best Buy have announced promotions on surplus big-ticket items. Despite a “supply chain crisis”, some companies have too much stuff?
 
Companies limited promotions during the pandemic because customers were willing to pay full price for things while quarantined. The high demand and easy sales have softened, leading to over-ordering that companies are now looking to offload with minimum losses.
 
Telling it like it is —  Next we’re gonna find out Costco has a bunch of extra EVs in the back room.
